orthosong

Scroll down for audio and examples. As a musician I'm sometimes tired of searching for chords and melodies, as for me the more fun part is manipulating them. The world around us luckily happens to be full of sequences, which can be easly interpreted as a melody provided a basic set of rules. As a bioinformatician and evolutionary biologist, I work with sequences constantly, originating from biological macromolecules (DNA, RNA and proteins). These sequences with their different respective codes (4 nucleotides for DNA and RNA, 20 amino acids for proteins) can be readily interpreted as linear melodies, providing the horizontal musical progress for a piece. This actually has been done before and one of the first persons to interpret biological macromolecules as musical information was the great evolutionairy biologist Susumu Ohno, mainly known for his seminal work in molecular evolution and the study of the influence of gene duplications on evolution (his classic, but rarely read, book Evolution by Gene Duplication (1970) is dedicated to this matter, see for example Hahn (2009) for a commentary on Ohno's magnum opus[1]).

Ohno's musical interpretation of genetic codes was not just playful sidenote in his research, he was really fascinated about the resulting pieces of music. He noted for example that a translation of the SARC oncogene (genes known to be involved in initiating and accelerating cancers) sounded quite like the funeral mars of Chopin: "It was sad, somber, and somewhat out of control, Really amazing.". In contrast, the song of a human antibody that breaks down the toxic chemical hydroxynitrolphenol turned out to be, "livelier, more chipper, helpful". Also when converting compositions of great composers to genetic code, he noted that the resuling stretches of coding sequences resembled known genes, for example for the Chopin F-Minor Nocturne, Opus 55, No. 1, he says that it reproduced the structure of the RNA polymerase II. He was fascinated by repetition, periodicity and endless cycles, he regarded these phenomena as the essence of life on earth (see this article and this article for example). But he went quite far with his thoughts on the relationship between music and genes and things start to sound like some pythagorean cult (read this).

At this moment I'm not (yet) really convinced of wether it is meaningful to search for relationships between music and (molecular) evolution, however it is at least an interesting notion and I consider giving it further thought. Nevertheless, the sequences still can be interpreted musically, and therefore may guide artistic processes, in a similar way the I Ching (book of changes) guided the musical composition of John Cage. Cage used the I Ching to compose music free of his personal tastes and intuitions, entirely originating from chance operations, "imitating nature in its manner of operation" in his own words. In a very similar way, and even more directly imitating nature, biological sequences can be used for making musical compositions, with the resulting music reflecting millions of years of evolution through random mutation and natural selection. (Note: this is under the assumption that my personal choice of gene families is not systematically linked with a certain sequence (and therefore musical) pattern, an assumption of which Ohno would doubt the validity).

It seems that every student in molecular biology or bioinformatics that has affinity with music comes up with the idea to convert biological sequences to music one time (it even has a wikipedia page), see also the footnotes below. However I wanted to give it a shot as well. Also most approaches I've encountered of making biological sequence inspired music are inspired on single linear sequences, resulting in the horizontal melodic aspect of music. However by combining orthologous genes that form a gene family (i.e. closely related sequences that are derived from a same common ancestral gene), a vertical (harmonic) musical structure can be inferred, namely by generating multiple sequence alignments (where columns represent simultaneously sounding notes i.e. chords). This results in a generative piece of music in both the vertical and horizontal dimension, visualized by the multiple sequence alignment. Note that multiple sequence alignments are often used to compute phylogenetic trees, which happens to be an aesthetically pleasing representation to guide the music. The basic approach adopted to make music with these alignments is to define a set of rules translating amino acids or nucleotides to notes, convert the multiple sequence alignment to a "score" and than generate a MIDI file with that score. orthosong is available here.

The all pervasive principle of repetitious recurrence governs not only coding sequence construction but also human endeavor in musical composition. "Organisms which have evolved on this earth are governed by multitudes of periodicities; tomorrow is another today, and the next year is going to be much like this year. Accordingly, the principle of repetitious recurrence pervades every aspect of life on this earth. Thus, individual genes in the genome have been duplicated and triplicated often to the point of redundancy, and each coding sequence consists of numerous variously truncated as well as variously base-substituted copies of the original primordial building block base oligomers and their allies. This principle even appears to govern the manifestations of human intellect; musical compositions also rely on this principle of repetitious recurrence. Accordingly, coding base sequences can be transformed into musical scores using one set rule. Conversely, musical scores can be transcribed to coding base sequences of long open reading frames." - Ohno & Ohno - Immunogenetics. 1986;

Repetition as the essence of life on this earth: music and genes. "In prebiotic nucleic acid replication, templates appear to have been in short supply. A single round of tandem duplication of existing oligomers assured progressive extension of templates to the length adequate for encoding of polypeptide chains. Thus, the first set of coding sequences had to be repeats of base oligomers encoding polypeptide chains of various periodicities. On one hand, the readiness of these periodical polypeptide chains to assume alpha-helical and/or beta-sheet secondary structures contributed to the extremely rapid initial functional diversification of these polypeptide chains. It would be recalled that most, if not all, of the sugar-metabolizing enzymes had already achieved the inviolable functional competence before the division of prokaryotes from eukaryotes. On the other hand, a certain (dipeptidic?) of the peptidic periodicities was apparently chosen as the timekeeping unit by the biological clock. Musical compositions too apparently evolved originally as a timekeeping device. Accordingly, repetitiousness is evident in all musical compositions. Evolution of musical compositions from the early Baroque to the late Romantic parallels that of coding sequences from rather exact repeats of base oligomers to more complex modern coding sequences in which repetitious elements are less conspicuous and more varied. Inasmuch as the earth is governed by the hierarchy of periodicities (days, months and years), such reliance on periodicities is rather expected." - Ohno S. Haematol Blood Transfus. 1987
